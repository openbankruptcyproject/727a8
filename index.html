Section 727(a)(8) - The 8-Year Chapter 7 Discharge Bar

What is Section 727(a)(8)?

Section 727(a)(8) of Title 11 of the United States Code is the federal statute that prevents a debtor from receiving a Chapter 7 bankruptcy discharge if they already received a Chapter 7 (or Chapter 11) discharge in a case filed within the preceding 8 years.

In plain English: if you filed Chapter 7 bankruptcy and received a discharge, you cannot get another Chapter 7 discharge until 8 full years have passed from the date you filed that earlier case.

The Statute

11 U.S.C. Section 727(a)(8): "The court shall grant the debtor a discharge, unless the debtor has been granted a discharge under this section in a case commenced within 8 years before the date of the filing of the petition."

This is one of several "discharge bars" in the Bankruptcy Code. It exists to prevent abuse of the bankruptcy system by people who would otherwise file Chapter 7 repeatedly to eliminate debts. The 8-year window was established by the Bankruptcy Abuse Prevention and Consumer Protection Act of 2005 (BAPCPA), which extended the prior 6-year bar.

The Filing-Date-to-Filing-Date Rule

The most important detail about Section 727(a)(8) is how the 8-year period is measured. The clock does not start when you received your discharge. It starts when you filed the prior case.

Here is exactly how it works:

  1. Start date: The date you filed your prior Chapter 7 petition (the petition date on the court's docket).
  2. End date: Add exactly 8 years to that filing date.
  3. New filing: You can file your new Chapter 7 case on or after that 8-year anniversary.

Example

If you filed Chapter 7 on March 15, 2018, and received your discharge on June 20, 2018, the 8-year bar expires on March 15, 2026 - not June 20, 2026. You could file a new Chapter 7 on March 15, 2026 or any date after.

This distinction matters because Chapter 7 cases typically take 3 to 5 months from filing to discharge. The filing-date rule means you become eligible months earlier than you would if the discharge date controlled.

Why Filing Date, Not Discharge Date?

The statute says "a case commenced within 8 years before the date of the filing of the petition." A case is "commenced" when the petition is filed under 11 U.S.C. Section 301. So both the start and end of the measurement are filing dates. This is well-established law and has been confirmed by courts across the country.

What Triggers the Bar

Section 727(a)(8) is triggered only when the prior discharge was granted under "this section" - meaning Section 727, which governs Chapter 7 discharges. The bar also applies if the prior discharge was under Section 1141 (Chapter 11 individual discharge), because the statute was amended by BAPCPA to include Chapter 11 discharges.

The bar is not triggered by:

  • A prior Chapter 13 discharge (that falls under Section 727(a)(9) instead, with a 6-year bar)
  • A prior Chapter 12 discharge (also under Section 727(a)(9))
  • A prior case that was dismissed without a discharge
  • A prior case where the debtor did not receive a discharge (denied, waived, or revoked)

Who Raises the Issue

The 727(a)(8) bar is not automatically enforced by the court. Someone must object. Under Federal Rule of Bankruptcy Procedure 4004(a), a complaint objecting to discharge must be filed within 60 days after the first date set for the 341(a) meeting of creditors.

In practice, the most common parties to raise this objection are:

  • The Chapter 7 trustee - who reviews the debtor's schedules and prior filing history
  • The U.S. Trustee - who monitors cases for abuse
  • Individual creditors - who have standing to object under Section 727(c)(1)

If nobody objects within the deadline, the debtor will receive a discharge even if the 8-year bar technically applied. However, this is rare in practice because PACER records and credit reports make prior filings easy to identify, and the U.S. Trustee's office routinely screens for this.

The BAPCPA Change: From 6 Years to 8 Years

Before BAPCPA took effect on October 17, 2005, the bankruptcy discharge bar rules was only 6 years. BAPCPA extended it to 8 years as part of a broader package of reforms intended to reduce bankruptcy abuse. This means:

  • Cases filed before October 17, 2005: the old 6-year rule applies.
  • Cases filed on or after October 17, 2005: the 8-year rule applies.

The relevant date is when the new case is filed, not when the prior case was filed. If you are filing a new Chapter 7 today, the 8-year rule applies regardless of when your prior case was filed.

What If You Are Inside the 8-Year Window

If fewer than 8 years have passed since your prior Chapter 7 filing date, you have several options:

  1. Wait. The simplest approach. Use our date calculator to find your exact eligibility date.
  2. File Chapter 13 instead. Section 727(a)(8) only bars a Chapter 7 discharge. You can file Chapter 13 at any time, though Section 1328(f) may limit your Chapter 13 discharge if you are within 4 years of your prior filing.
  3. File Chapter 7 without a discharge. In rare situations, a debtor may benefit from Chapter 7's automatic stay protection in bankruptcy even without receiving a discharge. This is uncommon and should only be considered with professional guidance.

Important Caveat

Filing a case you know will be barred from discharge can raise issues under Section 707(b) (abuse) and may result in sanctions. Consult a bankruptcy attorney before filing any case where discharge eligibility is in question.

Section 727(a)(8) vs. Other Discharge Bars

Section 727(a)(8) is just one of several time-based discharge bars in the Bankruptcy Code. Here is how they relate:

Prior Case New Case Waiting Period Statute
Chapter 7 Chapter 7 8 years 727(a)(8)
Chapter 13 Chapter 7 6 years* 727(a)(9)
Chapter 7 Chapter 13 4 years 1328(f)(1)
Chapter 13 Chapter 13 2 years 1328(f)(2)

*The 6-year bar under 727(a)(9) has exceptions if you paid 100% of unsecured claims, or paid at least 70% and the plan was proposed in good faith with best effort.

Common Mistakes and Misconceptions

Mistake 1: Counting from the Discharge Date

This is the single most common error. Debtors (and some attorneys) count 8 years from the date the discharge order was entered. The statute counts from the date the prior case was filed. Getting this wrong can cause a debtor to file too early and face an objection to discharge.

Mistake 2: Confusing Dismissed Cases with Discharged Cases

If your prior Chapter 7 case was dismissed - whether voluntarily or involuntarily - you did not receive a discharge. Section 727(a)(8) does not apply. You can file again immediately (subject to Section 109(g) restrictions on refiling after dismissal).

Mistake 3: Assuming the Bar is Automatic

The court does not automatically deny your discharge under 727(a)(8). An interested party must file a complaint. However, relying on nobody noticing is not a strategy - the U.S. Trustee screens for this and will object.

Mistake 4: Not Knowing About Chapter 13 as an Alternative

Many debtors who are inside the 8-year window for Chapter 7 can still get relief through Chapter 13. While the 1328(f) bar may limit your Chapter 13 discharge if you are within 4 years, you can still file Chapter 13 for the automatic stay and a structured repayment plan.

How to Check Your Eligibility

To determine whether Section 727(a)(8) bars your discharge, you need one piece of information: the filing date of your prior Chapter 7 case.

  1. Find your prior case filing date on PACER, your prior attorney's records, or your credit report.
  2. Add 8 years to that date.
  3. If today's date is on or after the 8-year anniversary, you are eligible.

Legislative History

The original Bankruptcy Reform Act of 1978 set the Chapter 7 discharge bar at 6 years. For over 25 years, debtors could file Chapter 7 every 6 years. BAPCPA, signed into law on April 20, 2005 and effective October 17, 2005, increased the bar to 8 years.

The legislative history of BAPCPA shows Congress was concerned about "serial filers" who used Chapter 7 repeatedly to shed debts. The 8-year bar was part of a package that also added the means test (Section 707(b)(2)), credit counseling requirements (Section 109(h)), and the new discharge bars in Section 1328(f) for Chapter 13 cases.

The extension from 6 to 8 years was not controversial on its own. Most of the BAPCPA debate focused on the means test and credit counseling requirements. The 727(a)(8) change passed as part of the larger bill with minimal separate discussion.

Practical Guidance

If you are considering filing Chapter 7 and have a prior bankruptcy on your record, here is what to do:

  1. Identify your prior case type and filing date. Chapter 7 triggers 727(a)(8). Chapter 13 triggers 727(a)(9).
  2. Calculate the bar window. Use our calculator or add 8 years manually.
  3. If you are within the window, consider Chapter 13 as an alternative, or wait for the window to expire.
  4. If you are outside the window, you are clear to file Chapter 7. No action needed on the 727(a)(8) front.
  5. Disclose everything. Question 9 on the bankruptcy petition asks about prior filings within the last 8 years. Answer honestly. Failure to disclose can result in denial of discharge under Section 727(a)(4) for fraud.
