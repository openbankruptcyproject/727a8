Planning Your Second Chapter 7 Filing

The 8-year bar under Section 727(a)(8) has expired, and you are considering filing Chapter 7 again. This page covers what to expect, how to prepare, and key differences from your first filing.

Confirm Your Eligibility

Step 1: Verify the 8-year window has passed

Use our date calculator to confirm. Remember: the period runs from filing date to filing date. If your prior Chapter 7 was filed on March 15, 2018, you can file again on or after March 15, 2026.

Beyond the 727(a)(8) window, you must also meet general Chapter 7 eligibility:

  • Means test: Your income must pass the means test under Section 707(b)(2), or your case must not constitute abuse of Chapter 7
  • Credit counseling: You must complete a credit counseling course within 180 days before filing (Section 109(h))
  • No Section 109(g) bar: If you had a case dismissed within the last 180 days under certain conditions, you may face a refiling bar
  • Good faith: Filing bankruptcy with the intent to abuse the system can result in dismissal

Key Differences from Your First Filing

1. You must disclose the prior filing

Question 9 on the bankruptcy petition asks about prior filings within the last 8 years. You must list your prior Chapter 7 case number, the court, and the filing date. Complete disclosure is essential - failure to disclose a prior filing can result in denial of discharge under Section 727(a)(4) for fraud.

2. Increased scrutiny is possible

The U.S. Trustee's office may examine your case more carefully when you have a prior bankruptcy. This is not a disqualification - it simply means your schedules and statements need to be accurate and complete.

3. Exemption laws may have changed

State exemption laws change over time. The exemptions available to you now may be different from those in your prior filing. Your attorney should review current exemption law for your state.

4. Your financial situation has changed

Eight years is a long time. Your income, assets, debts, and household composition are likely different. A fresh analysis is needed - do not assume the same approach from your prior filing will work.

Before You File Again

Consider the pattern

If you are filing Chapter 7 for a second time, it is worth examining what led you here again. Was it a specific crisis (medical emergency, job loss, divorce)? Or is there an underlying financial pattern that bankruptcy alone cannot fix? A second filing will discharge your current debts, but it will not prevent a third round of financial distress if the root causes are not addressed.

  • Consider whether bankruptcy is the right tool - debt negotiation, credit counseling, or other options may be appropriate
  • Address the underlying causes if possible - budgeting, emergency savings, insurance
  • Consult with a bankruptcy attorney - a second filing benefits from professional guidance even more than a first
  • Complete the required credit counseling before filing

The 8-Year Reset

When you file Chapter 7 again, a new 8-year clock starts. The 727(a)(8) bar will prevent another Chapter 7 discharge until 8 years after your new filing date. This also starts a new 4-year window for Section 1328(f)(1), which would bar a Chapter 13 discharge if you file Chapter 13 within 4 years.
